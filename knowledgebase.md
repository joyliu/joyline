# Do you think designers should specialize (e.g., UX, motion, research) or stay generalist? Why?

Entry ID: 6
Theme: Career Navigation, Mindset & Growth

## Related questions:

“Should I specialize or stay generalist?”, “Jack of all trades or master of one?”, “T-shaped designer vs specialist?”

## Context:

Designers often wrestle with whether to go deep in one specialty or spread their skills broadly. The industry keeps shifting, and what feels “safe” one year can look different the next.

## Reframe:

Right now, with tighter budgets and smaller teams, the most resilient designers are generalists who have depth in a couple of areas.

## Lesson hook:

Aim to be a generalist with spikes — broad enough to handle end-to-end work, but deep enough to be recognized for specific strengths.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

You don’t have to be a jack of all trades, master of none. Instead:

- **Be great at a few skills** — these are your superpowers and the tools you’ll always rely on.
- **Be good at most core skills** — so you can work efficiently across the end-to-end design process.
- **Be proficient in niche or cross-functional skills** — enough to “speak the language” with peers in research, engineering, or product strategy.

For example, my own mix: I go deep in systems thinking and design strategy. I’m solid in interaction and visual design, coding basics, product strategy, and research. That balance lets me lead projects without being a blocker.

The riskiest place to be is a specialist who needs constant coverage in other areas. If your manager has to pull extra people just to make you effective, your role becomes harder to justify.

## Lesson:

Don’t choose between specialist and generalist. Be a generalist with spikes: broad enough to contribute across the spectrum, but sharp enough in key areas to stand out.

# Do you want a hobby or a career?

Entry ID: 75
Theme: Career Navigation, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Many people are drawn to design because they enjoy it — and that’s great. But not everyone who enjoys design wants (or needs) to turn it into a career. The industry often blurs that line, especially with bootcamps selling the promise of high salaries.

## Reframe:

## Lesson hook:

A hobby can thrive on passion alone. A career requires both passion and skill.

To succeed in design as a career, you need two things:

1. **Passion** — genuine interest that keeps you curious and willing to put in the work.
2. **Marketable skill** — the craft and experience to deliver value in a professional setting.

## Lesson:

Ask yourself honestly: do you want to design for fun, or build a design career? Both are valid paths — but they require different levels of commitment.

# How do I balance designing for accessibility vs. shipping fast when timelines are tight?

Entry ID: 99
Theme: Design Strategy & Vision, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers ask this when they’re stuck between doing the right thing (designing inclusively) and meeting aggressive deadlines. It feels like accessibility always gets deprioritized, and they’re tired of being the only one fighting for it.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Should I sacrifice accessibility?” It’s “What’s the minimum viable accessibility I can ship now, and how do I build the case for more later?”

## Lesson hook:

Accessibility isn’t all-or-nothing. You can ship fast *and* inclusive if you know which standards are non-negotiable vs. nice-to-have.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The mistake people make is treating accessibility as a separate feature you add at the end. By then, it’s too expensive to fix. Instead, bake in baseline accessibility from the start, then prioritize deeper improvements based on your context.

Here’s how I think about it:

**1. Always ship these three (non-negotiable baseline):**

- **Color contrast** — WCAG AA minimum (4.5:1 for text). This is table stakes and costs nothing extra.
- **Keyboard navigation** — Tab order, focus states, escape to close. If someone can’t use a mouse, they should still be able to navigate.
- **Responsive/zoom-friendly layouts** — Don’t break at 200% zoom. Use flexible units, not fixed pixel widths.

If you ship without these, you’re not shipping an incomplete product — you’re shipping an *inaccessible* one. These should be part of your default design process, not a separate checklist.

**2. Prioritize beyond the baseline based on context:**

Ask three questions:

- **What stage is your company?** Early-stage startup proving product-market fit? Baseline is enough for now. Mature product with millions of users? You need to go deeper (screen reader support, ARIA labels, etc.).
- **Who’s your audience?** General consumers? Baseline covers most use cases. Government, healthcare, education? Accessibility is a legal requirement (Section 508, WCAG 2.1 AA). Build it in from day one or you’ll block the sale.
- **What’s your company culture?** Does your design system already handle accessibility at the component level? Is it part of requirements from the start? If yes, great — just use the system. If no, you’re swimming upstream. Start small: educate your PM on *why* it matters (legal risk, market expansion, user retention) and get one improvement into each sprint.

**3. When timelines are genuinely tight:**

Don’t skip accessibility — *phase* it. Ship the baseline now, then prioritize the rest:

- Sprint 1: Baseline (contrast, keyboard, zoom)
- Sprint 2: Screen reader support for critical flows
- Sprint 3: Advanced features (skip links, live regions, etc.)

This way you’re not blocking the launch, but you’re also not ignoring 15% of your potential users.

## Lesson:

Never ship without baseline accessibility (contrast, keyboard nav, zoom-friendly). Beyond that, prioritize based on company stage, audience, and culture. When time is tight, phase accessibility improvements — don’t skip them entirely.

# How do I build resilience in a market full of layoffs and reorgs?

Entry ID: 66
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

The tech industry has shifted dramatically since 2022–23. What used to feel like a stable, high-growth environment now feels unpredictable, with companies tightening budgets, reorganizing teams, and hiring far more cautiously. Designers aren’t just worried about job security — they’re trying to understand how to protect their long-term careers in a landscape that keeps changing.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I avoid layoffs?” It’s “How do I build long-term career resilience regardless of what the market does?”

## Lesson hook:

Resilience comes from anchoring your identity in your long-term career — not in any single job, title, or company. When you zoom out, a layoff or reorg becomes a chapter, not the story.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Let’s face it: the tech industry has changed. The high-growth, designer-frenzy era is over. Since the first big layoffs of 2022–23, companies have become much more disciplined about spending. Inflation, government policy shifts, and the rise of mature AI tools all play a role. When headcount opens, companies receive hundreds of strong applicants, and they can afford to be selective.

So what do you do in a market like this? **You focus on what you can control: your skills, your mental health, and your long-term career path.** Because when you look back years from now, this will likely be a small blip — not the defining moment of your career.

Here are the things that matter most:

1. **Keep your skills relevant and marketable.** Pay attention to what companies are actively hiring for. Align your portfolio with those skills. If you see gaps, make a plan to close them — courses, stretch projects, mentorship, anything that builds proof of capability.
2. **Keep your portfolio and resume up to date.** No one is “safe” anymore. An up-to-date portfolio is a form of psychological safety. Update it monthly or quarterly, not when panic hits.
3. **Understand what’s in your control (and what isn’t).** If your whole org is getting cut, that’s a business decision — not a reflection of your talent. Unless you’re on a PIP, layoffs usually have nothing to do with your performance.
4. **Strengthen your network now — not later.** If layoffs happen, the fastest route to a new role is through referrals. People inside the company can vouch for you in ways a cold portfolio never can.

**Pitfalls to avoid:**

- Reading too deeply into rumors — rumor spirals increase anxiety and distract from real work. Only act on validated signals.
- Over-functioning out of fear — burning yourself out will not save you from org decisions made above your level. Stay steady, not frantic.
- Neglecting your current performance because you’re scared — fear can create a self-fulfilling prophecy. Maintain your quality; don’t mentally exit before anything is official.
- Staying silent when leadership offers a forum — if there’s an AMA, all-hands, or open Q&A, ask what you need to ask. Leaders hold these sessions *because* they know uncertainty is high.

## Lesson:

Invest in skills that match today’s market, not yesterday’s. Keep your portfolio and resume continuously updated. Focus on what’s in your control — skills, reputation, network. Don’t internalize business decisions as a reflection of your worth. And use leadership communication moments to get clarity rather than fueling your own anxiety spiral.

# How do I deal with comparison when other designers seem ahead?

Entry ID: 72
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers often look around and feel behind. Someone got promoted faster. Someone worked on a high-visibility project. Someone got a better offer. Someone’s portfolio looks incredible. Comparison is one of the most common — and most corrosive — sources of stress in design careers.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I stop comparing myself?” It’s “How do I understand my own path without measuring it against someone else’s timeline?”

## Lesson hook:

Comparison only makes sense when the paths are identical — and in real life, they never are. You have different constraints, different opportunities, different timing, different managers, and different luck.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

First: stop the rat race.

Comparison is a game you can’t win because there will *always* be someone who looks further ahead in some dimension — skill, salary, opportunity, recognition, visibility. Chasing that is a fast lane to burnout.

But instead of just telling you to “stop comparing,” I want you to ask yourself a more honest question: **What’s motivating the comparison?** What exactly are you afraid of? And what do you think you’ll gain by proving you’re ahead?

Most comparison is emotional, not strategic.

The *only* productive time to compare yourself is during a job search — when your goal is to stand out among fellow candidates for the same role. In that context, you want to understand your superpower and position yourself clearly to an interview panel. Every other form of comparison? It drains your energy and offers no return.

Here’s why:

- You’re comparing their *highlight reel* to your *day-to-day reality*.
- You don’t know their circumstances.
- You don’t see the support system behind their wins.
- You don’t see the sacrifices they made to get there.
- You don’t see whether they’re actually happy.

Your job is not to match someone else’s timeline — it’s to understand your own.

**Focus on the inputs you can control: your skills, your knowledge, your craft, your relationships, your character.** Success follows from that, not from monitoring someone else’s LinkedIn updates.

And don’t forget to check your life goals. Some designers choose slower growth because of family, health, or personal passions. Others sprint for years because they have the capacity and desire to. Neither path is “better.” Your pace is right for where you are and what you want. Their pace is right for where they are and what *they* want.

## Lesson:

Comparison only makes sense when paths are identical — and they never are. Ask yourself *why* comparison is coming up; the trigger is usually emotional, not logical. Use comparison strategically only during job searches. Focus on what you can control — skills, knowledge, relationships, execution — and align your career pace with your life circumstances and values.

# How do I deal with stakeholders who constantly change requirements?

Entry ID: 31
Theme: Collaboration & Influence, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Having a moving target is frustrating and demoralizing. If your designs keep getting shelved or pivoted, it can feel like you’re wasting time.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I stop changes?” but “What’s driving the lack of alignment, and how can I address it?”

## Lesson hook:

Pixel-perfect screens don’t ship products — alignment does.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Frequent requirement changes usually point to one of a few root causes:

- **Lack of insights:** The team is shooting in the dark without enough research or evidence to commit.
- **Decision misalignment:** You may not be working with the true decision-maker. For example, a PM taking orders from leadership that changes direction on a whim.
- **Disempowered PM:** If your PM isn’t empowered to make decisions, requirements will stay fluid.
- **Poor documentation hygiene:** If requirements only live in tribal knowledge or hallway conversations, scope will constantly shift.

Once you identify the “why,” you can act:

1. **Clarify impacts.** Reframe requirement changes in terms of cost. What’s the business impact (delayed release, wasted effort)? The human impact (burnout, turnover risk)? The product impact (brittle, short-term fixes)? Frame these in terms leadership cares about: delivery risk, revenue, retention.
2. **Create structure.** Push for documentation, decision logs, or lightweight briefs so requirements don’t live only in verbal updates. As a designer, introduce artifacts like information architecture, journey maps, or user flows to anchor discussions.
3. **Facilitate alignment.** Run working sessions to define the problem, capture trade-offs, and secure commitments in the room. Written agreements reduce future churn.
4. **Be the help.** If your PM is disempowered, step in with insights or design structure to move things forward. Done well, this builds trust and positions design as the stabilizer in the room.
5. **Ask for help.** If shifting requirements are a repeated pattern, loop in your manager. Sometimes the root issue is a behavioral or process gap that needs escalation.

## Lesson:

You can’t eliminate shifting requirements, but you *can* uncover root causes, show the impact of constant pivots, and introduce structure. Your job isn’t to tolerate churn — it’s to spot patterns and help the org build better muscle for alignment. That’s how design graduates from service to strategy.

# How do I decide between freelancing and full-time roles?

Entry ID: 67
Theme: Career Navigation

## Context:

Designers often reach a point where they’re unsure whether to continue in full-time roles or switch to freelancing. The question usually comes up when they want more flexibility, feel stuck in a corporate structure, or wonder if contracting could accelerate their income. On the flip side, many early-career designers worry about losing mentorship or stability.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Which one is better?” It’s “Which path fits the way I like to work, live, and grow?”

## Lesson hook:

Freelancing gives you independence. Full-time roles give you infrastructure. You’re choosing not between “good and bad” but between two very different operating systems.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The decision between freelancing and full-time employment really comes down to your work style, lifestyle, and what stage of your career you’re in.

**Freelancing** gives you autonomy. You pick your clients, set your schedule, and define your process. But autonomy comes with responsibility. You’re not just a designer — you’re a business owner. You handle contracts, pricing, invoicing, client communication, taxes, and delivery. Some people love this blend of creative and entrepreneurial work. Others find it overwhelming.

If you need flexibility — caregiving, travel, health, or simply a non-9-to-5 rhythm — freelancing can be a great fit. But freelancing can also be isolating. You don’t automatically get teammates, mentors, or exposure to strong design leadership. **For early-career designers, this can slow your growth.** Being inside a team with senior designers, critiques, and cross-functional partners is invaluable in your foundational years.

**Full-time roles** trade autonomy for stability. You get a consistent salary, benefits, PTO, and a support structure that lets you focus on design rather than operations. You don’t need to think about where your next project comes from. You also get access to mentorship, teams, design systems, and organizational context that builds your professional maturity. The tradeoff is that you work within the company’s constraints — their process, their tools, their pace, their priorities. Your creative freedom sits inside a larger system.

There is no “right” choice. There is only the choice that aligns with your values, energy, and goals. Here’s a simple lens:

- **Choose freelancing if** you want independence, flexibility, and control — and you’re willing to run a business.
- **Choose full-time if** you want stability, growth infrastructure, and a team environment — and you’re okay operating within a larger system.

You can also move between the two throughout your career. Many designers do.

## Lesson:

Freelancing = independence; full-time = infrastructure. Know which you need right now. Freelancing requires you to run a business, not just design. Full-time roles offer stability, mentorship, and long-term career support. Early-career designers usually grow faster in full-time environments. Let your lifestyle, risk tolerance, and growth goals guide the decision.

# How do I evaluate whether a company truly values design?

Entry ID: 37
Theme: Career Navigation, Industry & Trends

## Context:

Every company claims to “care about design” on their job postings. But many use it as a buzzword without actually practicing user-centered work. Designers often wonder how to spot the difference *before* joining.

## Reframe:

The question isn’t “Does this company have good design?” — because glossy websites can be built by agencies. The real question is: “How does this company make decisions, and is design embedded in that process?”

## Lesson hook:

You can’t rely on aesthetics alone. The signals show up in how they prioritize, collaborate, and invest in design internally.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Look beyond the surface and use your conversations to probe:

- **Decision-making:** How do they decide what to work on? Are decisions grounded in user problems or just executive opinions?
- **Prioritization:** Do they weigh customer needs, insights, and impact — or do they jump on shiny requests?
- **Process:** Is exploration and iteration part of their workflow, or is design only brought in after requirements are set?
- **Language:** Do they mention research, frameworks, and design methodologies naturally, or is it all buzzwords?
- **Team structure:** Do they have in-house designers in meaningful roles, or is design outsourced?
- **Inclusion:** Are designers present early in the process, or only at the “make it pretty” stage?

Each answer is a signal of whether design is treated as strategy, craft, or decoration.

## Lesson:

Companies that truly value design invest in it as a capability, not a veneer. Pay attention to how they talk about problems, processes, and people — that will tell you more than any polished website.

# How do I facilitate cross-functional alignment when everyone has different priorities?

Entry ID: 58
Theme: Collaboration & Influence

## Context:

This question usually surfaces when teams are talking *at* each other, not *with* each other. Designers often feel caught in the middle — PMs chase deadlines, engineers focus on implementation efficiency, and leaders push top-down business goals. Everyone’s optimizing for their own definition of success, so progress stalls in loops of ‘talking’.

## Reframe:

The better question isn’t “How do I make everyone agree?” — it’s “How do I surface assumptions, create shared clarity, and help the group make good trade-offs together?”

## Lesson hook:

Alignment doesn’t mean agreement. It means shared understanding and clear next steps.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

When priorities clash, I treat alignment like a design problem — it needs structure, empathy, and facilitation.

1. **Treat directions as hypotheses.** Start by framing each proposal as a hypothesis, not a truth. “We believe this will help users because...” This reframes opinions as experiments and invites curiosity instead of defensiveness.
2. **Anchor on the shared “why.”** Bring everyone back to the core goal: *Why are we solving this problem? What outcome matters most?* Once the “why” is visible, “what” and “how” become easier to negotiate.
3. **Visualize the conflict.** As the visual thinker in the room, turn abstract debates into something tangible — a quick Miro or FigJam map showing trade-offs, impact, and effort. Seeing the problem reduces emotional heat and helps people reason instead of react.
4. **Facilitate, don’t dictate.** Ask neutral, framing questions that push the team toward decisions: “What’s the risk if we delay this?” “Which option unlocks more downstream impact?” “What would success look like in three months?” Good facilitation gives everyone a voice while guiding the group toward convergence.
5. **Document and revisit.** Summarize what was agreed, what’s still open, and who owns next steps. A short decision log keeps the team honest and prevents the dreaded “Wait, didn’t we decide that already?” Sometimes, due to timeline or technical constraints, you can only go with the second-best option. In those cases, make the trade-off explicit and ask your PM partner, “When would be the right time to revisit this?” That small question turns compromise into intention — and ensures good ideas don’t get lost, just parked for the right moment.
6. **Check alignment over time.** Alignment drifts — especially over long product cycles. I do quick pulse checks at milestones to confirm the team’s still rowing in the same direction.

## Lesson:

Facilitating alignment isn’t about forcing consensus; it’s about *designing clarity*. When you make trade-offs visible, anchor the team on shared goals, and document decisions, you transform chaos into coordinated action. And in doing so, you quietly build influence and trust across every function.

# How do I handle whiteboard/live design challenges?

Entry ID: 63
Theme: Portfolio & Interviewing

## Context:

Live design sessions can be nerve-wracking. You’re asked to think out loud, solve ambiguous problems, and show your process — all while being watched. Many designers question whether this practice is fair, especially for those who think best with time and space.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I impress them?” — it’s “How do I show my thinking clearly under ambiguity?”

## Lesson hook:

Hiring managers aren’t grading your drawings — they’re evaluating how you think, collaborate, and stay composed when the problem is unclear.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

As a hiring manager, I can tell you: we’re not expecting polished UI mockups in a whiteboard challenge. What we’re looking for is *how you work when the path is fuzzy* — because that’s the real job.

Here’s what we observe:

- **How you start.** What do you do when you first receive a request? Do you pause to clarify goals, users, and success criteria — or jump straight into solutions?
- **How you handle ambiguity.** When details are missing, do you freeze, over-design, or start asking good questions to reduce uncertainty?
- **The kinds of questions you ask.** Are you thinking about the system? The user? The constraints? The business? The questions reveal your design maturity more than your sketches do.
- **How you prioritize.** When there are multiple possible directions, how do you narrow focus? What trade-offs or frameworks guide your decisions?
- **How you communicate.** Can you explain your thinking clearly, narrate trade-offs, and engage your interviewer as a collaborator rather than an audience?

These are the same behaviors that make great designers effective at work — structured thinking, curiosity, and calm problem-solving under pressure.

**How to prepare:**

1. **Practice facilitation, not perfection.** Run mock sessions with friends or mentors. Practice guiding a conversation, not performing.
2. **Simulate pressure.** Record yourself solving a problem on a timer. Join whiteboard practice groups. Even improv classes help train your brain to stay fluid.
3. **Manage your nerves.** Find what grounds you — deep breathing, pacing, light humor — anything that helps you return to clarity in the moment.
4. **Let go of “right answers.”** Interviewers care far more about your reasoning than your final design. Treat the session as a *collaborative exploration*, not an exam.

## Lesson:

Whiteboard challenges aren’t about catching you off guard — they’re about seeing your process under real-world pressure. Focus less on being perfect and more on showing how you think, clarify, and co-create. Once you make peace with that, you’ll start leading the room instead of performing for it.

# How do I influence decisions when I don’t have authority?

Entry ID: 60
Theme: Collaboration & Influence

## Context:

Designers often sit at the crossroads of decisions without owning the final call. PMs control scope and timelines, engineers guard feasibility, and executives steer funding and strategy. That can make designers feel sidelined — especially when you see a better path forward but can’t seem to make others see it.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I get people to listen to me?” — it’s “How do I earn enough credibility and trust that my perspective shapes decisions, even without a title?”

## Lesson hook:

Influence is built, not bestowed. People follow clarity and consistency more than hierarchy.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Most of my career, I’ve operated without formal authority — but I’ve learned designers have leverage in subtler ways.

1. **Lead with evidence, not opinion.** Bring data, research, or pattern rationale that ties directly to user or business outcomes. Once you anchor the conversation in impact, you shift it from “my preference” to “what’s best for users and the product.” People argue less with evidence.
2. **Speak your partners’ language.** PMs think in metrics, engineers in feasibility, executives in risk and return. Translate your point of view into their vocabulary. That’s how design earns a strategic seat — by showing shared goals, not separate priorities.
3. **Co-create instead of convincing.** The most influential designers I know don’t “win arguments”; they run workshops, storyboards, or mapping sessions where everyone sees their fingerprints on the outcome. Shared ownership = shared alignment.
4. **Make progress visible.** Keep a steady cadence of updates, artifacts, and summaries. Influence compounds when others see your clarity turn into motion. Over-communicate decisions and how design advanced the work — that’s how you build institutional trust.
5. **Play the long game.** Be the partner who balances idealism with pragmatism. Push for the right thing, but also flex when timelines tighten. Over time, that consistency makes people seek your input early — which is the truest form of influence.

## Lesson:

You don’t need authority to lead — but you do need credibility, empathy, and follow-through. The designer who helps the team move forward, clarifies trade-offs, and delivers reliably becomes the quiet center of gravity in every room. That’s how real influence works.

# How do I know if I’m burned out or just bored?

Entry ID: 70
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers often hit a point where they feel “off.” They’re unmotivated, dragging their feet, or feeling disconnected from their work — but they can’t tell whether they’re **burned out** or simply **bored**. Since both show up as low energy, it’s easy to confuse them. Understanding the difference is key to choosing the right next step.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Am I burned out or bored?” It’s “What is my energy trying to tell me — and what needs to change?”

## Lesson hook:

Burnout drains your energy. Boredom drains your growth. The emotions are different, but the solution starts with noticing the signals early.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Burnout and boredom might feel similar on the surface — low motivation, procrastination, or a sense of “I don’t want to do this.” But the internal experience is very different, and knowing which one you’re facing helps you take the right next step.

A simple way to start is to check in with your emotions *before* you begin your workday — before you open your laptop or join a meeting. Early cues are usually the most honest.

**If you’re burned out**, the feelings sound like: “I’m exhausted.” “There’s too much on my plate — I can’t keep up.” “I’m overwhelmed and depleted.” Burnout is often workload-based, people-based (difficult stakeholders, constant context switching), or pressure-based. You’re not outgrowing the work — you’re being drained by it.

**If you’re bored**, the feelings sound more like: “I’ve outgrown this work.” “Nothing here challenges me anymore.” “I don’t feel excited or stretched.” Boredom shows up when your growth has stalled — your skills are ahead of the work you’re doing.

Once you can name the emotion, you can take action.

**If you’re burned out**, ask yourself what’s causing the overload. Is it schedule-based? You need prioritization support. Is it people-based? You need air cover or escalation help. Is it project-based? Too much ambiguity or too many parallel tracks? Be proactive with your manager. Bring a plan, not just a problem: “What do we deprioritize?” “Where can we reduce meetings?” “Can we redistribute responsibilities?”

**If you’re bored**, explore where you can grow next — a different project, a new problem space, shadowing or pairing with another designer, taking on more complex scope, or discussing promotion readiness if your level no longer matches your capability. Boredom isn’t a failure — it’s a signal that you’ve successfully mastered your current level.

Design work is creative problem-solving, which means your energy is one of your most important tools. The sooner you can identify whether the problem is **depletion** or **stagnation**, the sooner you can make the shift you need.

## Lesson:

Burnout = depleted energy. Boredom = stalled growth. Check in with your emotions before starting your workday — that’s where the real signals appear. If you’re burned out, pinpoint the source and bring a plan to your manager. If you’re bored, seek new challenges, scope, or career direction. Both states mean something needs to change — and you have options.

# How do I price my design work fairly as a freelance designer?

Entry ID: 93
Theme: Career Navigation

## Context:

## Reframe:

## Lesson hook:

Pricing isn’t about self-worth. It’s about supply, demand, and covering your costs.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Pricing isn’t about guessing what you’re worth — it’s about running the numbers and understanding your position in the market.

Here’s the math:

1. **Calculate your baseline.** What do you need to earn to make this sustainable? If freelancing is your main income, factor in: living costs, taxes (usually 25–30%), health insurance, retirement, unpaid admin time, software/tools, and slow months. If it’s a side gig, your baseline is lower — you’re buying flexibility and learning, not covering rent.
2. **Research market rates.** What are designers at your skill level charging in your area? Check communities, ask other freelancers, look at public rate sheets. Junior designers with thin portfolios might charge $50–75/hr. Mid-level with solid case studies: $100–150/hr. Senior specialists with reputations: $150–250+/hr. ## Lesson:

Start with research, calculate your baseline, then adjust based on your positioning. If clients consistently say yes without negotiation, raise your rates.

# How do I run a user interview effectively?

Entry ID: 97
Theme: Collaboration & Influence, Portfolio & Interviewing

## Context:

Designers ask this when they’ve been handed their first research study — or when they’ve done interviews before but felt like the insights were scattered and hard to use. The anxiety is usually: “I don’t know what I’m doing and I’ll waste users’ time.”

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I conduct an interview?” It’s “How do I design a focused conversation that gets me the insight I actually need?”

## Lesson hook:

A user interview is only as good as the question you’re trying to answer before you walk in the room.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

User interviews are one of the most valuable tools in a designer’s toolkit — but only if you go in with a clear purpose. Here’s how to run one well:

**Before the session:**

1. **Define your learning objective.** What do you actually need to know? This single question shapes everything else: your script, your recruits, your synthesis. The biggest mistake junior designers make is treating interviews like a fishing expedition — asking everything because they’re finally in front of a user. Resist this. A focused study with 5 good questions beats a sprawling one with 20.
2. **Write your script deliberately.** Once you know your objective, write questions that serve it. Study basic survey and interview design principles: avoid leading questions (“Don’t you find it frustrating when...?”), avoid closed questions (yes/no answers that kill conversation), and keep questions open-ended (“Tell me about a time when...”). Keep your script contained and cohesive — every question should connect back to your learning objective.
3. **Recruit the right people.** Who has the experience or behavior you’re trying to understand? A study with the wrong participants gives you confident, useless data.

**During the session:**

1. **Set the stage.** Introduce yourself and the purpose of the session. Encourage them to think out loud and be honest — there are no wrong answers. Ask for permission to record if you need it.
2. **Stay consistent.** Run each session the same way. If you go off-script for one participant, you’ve introduced a variable that makes synthesis harder. It’s okay to probe deeper on interesting answers, but return to your script.
3. **Leave space.** This one trips people up. Don’t fill silence with more questions or small talk. Let participants think. The best insights often come after a pause.

**After the session:**

1. **Capture highlights immediately.** Don’t wait. Jot down the moments that stood out while they’re fresh. Then, once you’ve run all your sessions, synthesize across them to find themes and patterns. One person saying something is anecdote. Three people saying it is a signal.

## Lesson:

Start with your learning objective and let everything else follow from it. A well-scoped interview with a tight script and the right participants will give you more useful insight than a long, wandering session with a dozen questions.

# How do I run an effective design review?

Entry ID: 39
Theme: Collaboration & Influence, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Asking for feedback and alignment is a critical part of a designer’s day-to-day. It’s not just about creating — it’s about ensuring your concept lands with peers and stakeholders, and that it’s executed well.

## Reframe:

The better question isn’t “How do I get feedback?” but “How do I create a productive forum that validates whether my design is on the right track?”

## Lesson hook:

Design reviews work best when they’re a safe space to fail early, learn fast, and course-correct together.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

**Before the review:**

1. **Set scope.** What exactly are you reviewing? A flow, a pattern, a high-fidelity mock? Clear criteria keeps feedback focused.
2. **Provide context.** Frame the problem, why it matters, and why now. Without context, reviewers will default to surface-level feedback.
3. **Guide the ask.** Tell people what kind of input you’re looking for — usability, alignment with system, narrative clarity, etc. Otherwise, feedback will scatter.

**During the review:**

1. **Track feedback.** Use a note-taker, AI tool, or co-facilitator so you can focus on discussion.
2. **Facilitate actively.** Clarify feedback in the moment — *“Can you elaborate on what feels confusing?”* — to uncover the underlying concern.
3. **Balance voices.** Watch out for pile-ons. Steer the group away from design-by-committee by asking, *“Let’s pause — what’s the key trade-off here?”*

**After the review:**

1. **Synthesize.** All feedback is valid as input, but not all of it requires action. Sort into “act now,” “consider later,” and “out of scope.”
2. **Decide and communicate.** Show reviewers how their input influenced the next iteration. Closing the loop builds trust.

## Lesson:

A strong design review isn’t just about collecting opinions. It’s about framing the problem, guiding the discussion, and synthesizing feedback into clear next steps. The more you practice facilitation, the more trust and influence you’ll build across your team.

# How do I scale design reviews across multiple product teams?

Entry ID: 94
Theme: Org Design & Ops

## Context:

It’s always expensive to get multiple people into the room (in person or virtual). The clock is ticking and money burning. You want the meeting to be valuable. The challenge: how do you make sure teams have visibility to each other’s work, ensure leadership is giving feedback early enough to shape strategy and unblock teams, and also ensure that topics are relevant to each participating team.

## Reframe:

How do I ensure the right people are in the room to review relevant topics so that we avoid too many cooks in the kitchen but also don’t design in silos?

## Lesson hook:

Scaling isn’t about bigger meetings — it’s about designing the right forums for the right feedback.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The problem with scaling design reviews is that most teams default to “invite everyone to everything.” You end up with 15 people on a Zoom where 3 actually need to be there, and the other 12 are multitasking or wondering why they’re in the room.

Here’s the system I use:

1. **Separate visibility from decision-making.** Most people need to *see* work (for context and alignment), but far fewer need to *critique* it. Don’t conflate these two needs in the same meeting.
2. **Create tiers based on what you need:**
    - **Team critiques** (weekly, 30–45 min): Deep craft feedback with your immediate squad. 4–6 people max. This is where you iterate on details.
    - **Cross-team showcases** (bi-weekly, 30 min): Demo finished work or key decisions. No live critique — just “here’s what we shipped and why.” This is for visibility and learning, not feedback.
    - **Leadership reviews** (monthly, 60 min): Strategic alignment with directors/VPs. Focus on: Are we solving the right problems? Does this fit our roadmap? Not about button colors.
3. **Make async your default for awareness.** Use Slack, Figma comments, or recorded walkthroughs for broad visibility. Save sync time for conversations that actually need real-time discussion. If someone can leave feedback in a comment, they don’t need to be in the meeting.
4. **Be explicit about what each forum is for.** When you send a calendar invite, say: “This is a team critique — bring questions on interaction details” vs. “This is a showcase — no feedback expected, just visibility.” People appreciate knowing why they’re there.

The teams that struggle here either run giant critique-by-committee meetings (too many cooks) or design in total isolation (then get blindsided in leadership reviews). You need both tight feedback loops and broad visibility — just don’t try to do both in the same room.

## Lesson:

Design a tiered system: team critiques for craft, cross-team showcases for visibility, leadership reviews for strategy. Use async for broad awareness, sync for deep feedback.

# How do I spot when a team’s culture is quietly slipping?

Entry ID: 90
Theme: Org Design & Ops

## Context:

Leaders ask this when something feels off but they can’t name it. The team isn’t in crisis, but the energy has shifted. They want to catch it before it becomes a real problem.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Is my team struggling?” It’s “What are the early warning signs that culture is eroding — and what do I do about it?”

## Lesson hook:

Culture doesn’t collapse overnight — it slips quietly through small behavioral changes you can spot if you’re paying attention.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Culture erosion happens gradually. You won’t see people yelling or walking out — instead, you’ll see disengagement creep in through subtle shifts in behavior.

Here are the signals I watch for:

1. **Meetings feel different.** People used to debate and push back; now they’re quiet and compliant. Fewer hands go up when you ask for volunteers. Conversations feel transactional: “What do you need from me?” instead of “What if we tried this?” The energy in the room is flat.
2. **People do the minimum.** They’re completing their assigned tasks but nothing more. No one’s proposing new ideas, offering to help teammates, or taking initiative on side projects. They clock in, do the work, clock out.
3. **Strategic thinking disappears.** Conversations shift from “Where are we headed in six months?” to “What’s due this week?” People stop talking about industry trends, career growth, or long-term vision. Everything becomes short-term and reactive.
4. **1:1s become status updates.** Instead of using 1:1 time to talk about growth, blockers, or team dynamics, people just report on task progress. They’re managing up instead of being honest about what’s bothering them.
5. **Private conversations increase.** People are talking to each other in DMs and side channels instead of in public forums. Or worse, they’re talking to recruiters and other teams about opportunities elsewhere.

**What to do when you spot these signs:**

Don’t ignore it and hope it fixes itself. It won’t.

1. **Name it directly.** In your next team meeting or 1:1, say: “I’ve noticed the energy feels different lately. Meetings are quieter, and it feels like we’re just checking boxes. What’s going on?” Give people permission to be honest.
2. **Ask better questions.** In 1:1s, stop asking “How’s work?” and start asking: “What’s frustrating you right now? What would make this team better? If you could change one thing, what would it be?”
3. **Look for systemic issues.** Culture doesn’t slip because people are lazy — it slips because something in the system is broken. Are people overworked? Is leadership thrashing on priorities? Did a recent reorg create confusion? Fix the root cause, not the symptoms.

## Lesson:

Watch for quiet disengagement: flat meeting energy, minimum effort, short-term thinking, transactional 1:1s, and private conversations. When you spot it, name it directly and dig for the systemic issue causing it.

# How do I talk about salary expectations without underselling myself?

Entry ID: 92
Theme: Career Navigation

## Context:

Designers ask this when they’re scared of pricing themselves out of a role or leaving money on the table. The stakes feel high — get it wrong and you either lose the offer or underpay yourself for years.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “What number should I say?” It’s “How do I negotiate confidently within the system that already exists?”

## Lesson hook:

Salary negotiation isn’t about convincing someone you’re worth more — it’s about understanding the range and positioning yourself within it.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

First, understand how compensation actually works. For mid-to-large companies, every open headcount has a pre-approved salary range. For example:

- Mid-level designer: $120K-$150K
- Senior designer: $150K-$190K
- Staff designer: $190K-$240K

Your offer will land somewhere in that range based on your experience, location, and how you interview. Hiring managers discuss whether you’re “lower end,” “middle,” or “upper end” of the band. It’s rare to get paid outside the range — if your expectation is way above, the conversation usually shifts to whether you’re actually interviewing for the right level.

Here’s how to use this knowledge:

1. **Do your research before the conversation.** Know what designers at your level make in your market. Use [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/), Glassdoor, or ask peers. If the range for your level in your city is $130K-$160K, don’t anchor at $180K unless you have a strong reason.
2. **Delay the number as long as possible.** If a recruiter asks early, say: “I’d love to learn more about the role and scope first. What’s the range you have budgeted for this position?” Make them give you the range. If they push, give a wide range based on your research: “Based on my experience and market rates, I’m looking at $140K-$170K, but I’m flexible depending on the full package.”
3. **Negotiate with leverage.** Companies expect you to negotiate — it’s built into the process. The strongest leverage points are competing offers (“I have another offer at $X, but I prefer your company. Can you match?”), unique skills (“I bring 5 years of experience in fintech design, which is directly relevant to this role”), and total comp, not just base — ask about equity, bonuses, signing bonuses, and PTO. Sometimes they can’t move on base but can add a $10K signing bonus.
4. **Use clear, confident language.** Don’t apologize or hedge. Say: “Based on my research and experience, I’m looking for $155K” not “I was hoping for maybe around $155K if that’s okay?”

If they come back lower than you want, don’t immediately accept. Say: “I was hoping for closer to $X. Is there flexibility on base salary, or can we explore equity or a signing bonus?”

## Lesson:

Research the market range for your level, ask the company for their range first, then negotiate confidently using competing offers or unique strengths. Never accept the first offer — companies expect you to counter.

Designers ask this when they’ve watched their carefully crafted design get built wrong — colors off, spacing inconsistent, interactions missing. The gap between “what I designed” and “what got shipped” feels frustrating and avoidable.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I write better docs?” It’s “How do I communicate design intent in a way that matches how engineers actually work?”

A good handoff isn’t about perfecting the document — it’s about aligning your process with engineering’s workflow.

Here’s the system I use:

1. **Learn how your engineers work first.** Before you write anything, ask: How do they break down work? Most engineering teams work in agile with 2-week sprints and development stories (tickets). Each story is a small, shippable piece of work. If you organize your designs by feature across multiple surfaces but they build page-by-page, your handoff will be a mess.
2. **Match your Figma structure to their tickets.** If engineering is building “Search page” then “Results page” then “Detail page,” organize your frames and annotations the same way. Make it obvious which designs map to which tickets. Use frame names that mirror their story titles.
3. **Understand their design literacy.** Some engineers are Figma-fluent and will jump into Dev Mode to grab specs. Others eyeball it and guess. If your team isn’t design-tool savvy, you need more explicit annotations: exact spacing values, component states, edge cases. If they’re experienced, you can rely more on inspection tools and less on red-line markup.
4. **Include the essentials, skip the fluff.** Every handoff doc should have: happy path + edge cases (empty states, loading, errors, long text), interaction notes (What happens on click? What triggers this state?), responsive behavior (How does this adapt on mobile? What breaks first?), and component mapping (Use “Button/Primary” from the design system, not a custom comp). Skip design rationale essays, user research summaries, and mood boards. Engineers need build instructions, not context.
5. **Do a walkthrough, don’t just drop a link.** Schedule 30 minutes to walk through the designs live. Answer questions in real-time. This catches 80% of potential misalignments before they become bugs.
6. **Stay engaged during implementation.** Check in at the midpoint of the sprint. Look at staging builds before QA. If something’s off, catch it early when it’s cheap to fix.

## Lesson:

Match your handoff structure to engineering’s workflow. Include interaction details and edge cases. Walk through designs live instead of dropping a Figma link. Stay engaged during implementation — don’t disappear after handoff.

Theme: Design Strategy & Vision

## Lesson hook:

Strategy without delivery is just theory. Delivery without strategy is technical debt.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The question isn’t about perfectionism; it’s about reconnecting designers to craftsmanship and self-respect in the work they sign their name to.

## Lesson:

Safety isn’t about comfort; it’s about trust under pressure. When designers know expectations are clear, feedback is fair, and quality is tied to pride, they’ll take bolder creative risks — and that’s where excellence thrives.

# How do you handle burnout in design?

Entry ID: 61
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Burnout hits designers harder than we expect because our work demands both creativity and collaboration. Between shifting priorities, endless meetings, and constant feedback loops, it’s easy to confuse exhaustion with commitment. Mentees often ask this when they feel drained but worry that taking a break will look like weakness.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I push through burnout?” — it’s “How do I recognize the signs early and design my workload, habits, and support system to stay healthy?”

## Lesson hook:

Burnout isn’t a badge of honor — it’s a signal that something in the system needs redesign.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Burnout is real. Let’s start there.

Work will always be there — there’s always another release, another stakeholder, another “urgent” request. The key is knowing how to manage your limits before they manage you.

I look at burnout through two lenses: **capacity** and **focus.**

1. **Be honest about your capacity.** Learn to say no — early and clearly. Most burnout I’ve seen comes not from overwork alone, but from unclear boundaries. If you’re stretched too thin, ask yourself: What’s essential vs. optional? Which projects can I delegate or pause? Have I told my manager the truth about my bandwidth? Asking for help isn’t weakness — it’s a sign of self-awareness and strength.
2. **Audit where your time actually goes.** Review your calendar and week objectively: how much time are you spending in meetings, doing heads-down work, or learning? If you’re a junior or mid-level designer spending too much time in meetings, you’re probably not leaving enough focus time for design craft. If you’re a senior designer who’s skipping cross-functional check-ins, you’ll end up duplicating work and firefighting later. A quick weekly time audit helps you see where you’re leaking energy.
3. **Know the signs — and catch them early.** I’ve heard teammates say, *“My brain is mush. I can’t think anymore.”* BOOM — that’s a sign of burnout. I know that feeling very well too, so I know exactly what they mean. For me, if I find myself rereading the same email three times or hitting a creative block I can’t break, that’s my signal. When that happens, I go for a walk, exercise, or sleep on it and come back the next day. Reset before your system forces you to.
4. **Protect focus and recovery.** Decline meetings you’re not required in. Block design time. Take real breaks — not “eat lunch at your desk” breaks. And take vacations *before* you’re on the edge, not after.
5. **Ask for help early.** Some designers hesitate to ask for help because they think stretching past their limit will prove they’re “promotion ready.” But the opposite is true. If I start getting pings from PMs about missed deadlines or stalled projects, it signals you didn’t flag the problem early — and that’s a trust issue. I wouldn’t support that person for promotion because readiness means knowing your boundaries, not hiding them. Hitting your limit isn’t bad — it shows you exactly where your current boundary is. Once you know that, we can work together to expand it safely.
6. **Model boundaries.** As a leader, I don’t want to be the reason someone feels pressured to overwork. I schedule messages for work hours, take full PTO, and encourage others to do the same. Culture changes when leaders model sustainability.
7. **Reframe burnout recovery as process improvement.** When someone burns out, I don’t treat it as personal failure — I treat it as a *system failure.* Something about our processes, staffing, or clarity broke. The fix isn’t just self-care; it’s structural care.

## Lesson:

Burnout doesn’t mean you’re weak — it means you’ve been running at full power for too long without recharge. Audit your time, protect your focus, and ask for help before exhaustion turns into damage. A healthy designer delivers more impact, not fewer hours.

# How do you handle performance management for underperforming designers?

# How do you introduce design ops practices without slowing teams down?

Entry ID: 50
Theme: Org Design & Ops

## Context:

Design Ops often gets a bad rap — designers fear it’ll add red tape and slow their flow, while leadership sees it as a path to consistency and scalability. Mentees ask this when they’re tasked with bringing structure to a fast-moving org but worry about killing creative energy or trust.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I enforce process?” — it’s “How do I design systems that remove friction, not add it?”

## Lesson hook:

Ops succeeds when it feels invisible — like infrastructure that quietly makes everyone faster.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The worst thing you can do is roll out new processes *at* the team. Designers are already underwater. You have to meet them where they are — and make every new practice feel like a time-saver, not a chore.

1. **Start with empathy for designers’ reality.** If designers are already swamped, another “top-down directive” will backfire. I first observe where friction actually lives: Is it in reviews? QA? Visibility? Then I design Ops around those pain points, not what looks neat in a spreadsheet.
2. **Lead with *why* and *what’s in it for them.*** If there’s an action or tool they must use, always pair it with rationale. “We’re logging tickets so you can see all your projects in one dashboard — not for reporting’s sake.” Designers adopt faster when they understand how it helps *them*.
3. **Accompany top-down requirements with real support.** Sometimes Ops initiatives come from leadership or compliance needs. When that happens, I’m transparent: I explain what we need to abide by, *and* I bring tools that make it easier to comply. For example, when our org required all design work to go through an internal ticketing system, I didn’t just say “you must do this.” I built a dashboard so designers could easily track their tickets, created a quick-reference cheatsheet, and set up a calendar scheduler to book design reviews directly. The directive stayed, but the experience became painless.
4. **Automate and templatize wherever possible.** If something needs to happen repeatedly (handoffs, QA, stakeholder reviews), make templates and automated reminders do the work. Manual process equals resentment.
5. **Help the team see the map, not the manual.** Instead of handing designers a dense 10-step process doc, I create a *simplified checklist* so they can see the sequence at a glance. The worst mistake I’ve seen comes from program managers who push process without explaining *why* or understanding the existing workflow. The best way to manage change is to bring designers along — show the value behind the new system and how it improves their day-to-day.
6. **Invite feedback and iterate.** Treat Ops like a product. After rollout, ask: “What’s working? What’s annoying?” Designers will tell you exactly where friction hides — and fixing it together turns skeptics into champions.
7. **Frame Ops as empowerment, not policing.** My goal isn’t to control creativity; it’s to *protect designers’ time.* When Ops removes ambiguity, designers spend less time chasing answers and more time designing.

## Lesson:

Design Ops isn’t about enforcing process — it’s about creating clarity and speed. When designers feel supported instead of managed, process becomes muscle memory, not overhead. The best Ops work doesn’t slow people down — it lets them fly.

- The company shifts mission and you no longer connect.
- A re-org derails your trajectory.

## Lesson:

Design impact isn’t just what users see — it’s how the organization thinks.

# How do you protect team health when the org is under delivery pressure?

Every design manager eventually hits this moment: the org is sprinting, executives are pushing for delivery, and your team is running on fumes. The real question isn’t “How do I stop the pressure?” — it’s “How do I keep the team healthy and effective inside that pressure?”

## Lesson hook:

Pressure is inevitable. Burnout is optional.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

As a manager, I think of myself as a *temperature regulator* — not removing the heat, but keeping it from boiling over.

1. **Ensure clarity across everyone.** Ambiguity amplifies stress. When urgency spikes, I always start with: What are we actually being asked to do? What’s the underlying goal or risk? What’s due by when — and what’s negotiable? Most “emergencies” shrink once you expose them to clarity. Showing progress early often opens room for negotiation.
2. **Prioritize ruthlessly.** Don’t wait for others to define priorities — drive that alignment. When multiple PMs each have “Priority 1,” I consolidate requests into one shared spreadsheet and make the group choose. “If we could only deliver one thing well, what would it be?” This forces trade-offs into the open and prevents your team from silently absorbing impossible scope.
3. **Create buffers — for them and for yourself.** I aim for realistic plans with stretch goals rather than wishful plans with guaranteed burnout. I’ve learned the hard way that taking on too much leads to corner-cutting and blame cycles. A well-placed buffer turns “we’re doomed” into “we’re tight but safe.”
4. **Set boundaries and model them.** If I tell my team not to work nights but reply to Slack at 11 p.m., I’ve just set the wrong norm. I use scheduled send, protect weekends, and check twice if someone volunteers for overtime. Boundaries modeled by leaders become permission for others to rest.

7. **Be the Swiss-cheese shield.** Earlier in my career, I described managers as “shit umbrellas,” shielding the team from all chaos. Now I think of us more like *Swiss cheese shields*: mostly solid, but with intentional holes. I filter unnecessary noise, but I also let through enough context so the team stays connected to business reality. Overprotection breeds detachment — and that’s just another kind of risk.
8. **Escalate when needed.** Sometimes the pressure isn’t fixable within your span of control. If deadlines remain impossible, if product partners can’t agree on priorities, or if scope keeps shifting weekly, those are signs of *organizational dysfunction*, not team weakness. That’s when I escalate. I’ll ask my design director or VP for air cover — to help reset expectations, align scope, or broker a conversation at their level. Escalation isn’t blame; it’s boundary-setting.

## Lesson:

You can’t remove pressure, but you can design how it flows. Clarity, prioritization, boundaries, and recovery are your tools. When your team sees you absorb stress with calm, communicate trade-offs transparently, and defend time to think — they learn that health isn’t a luxury; it’s a leadership strategy.

A clear process earns trust before the work even starts.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

I explain my design process as a series of steps: clarifying the problem, exploring ideas, aligning on a solution, validating, building, and learning.

1. **Clarify the problem.** I always start with the *what* and the *why*: What’s the problem? Who’s experiencing it? Why does it matter now? What’s the motivation and timeframe? Who cares about this problem, and what resources do we have to solve it? This step is about grounding everyone before we run to solutions.
2. **Explore ideas.** I like to make ideas tangible quickly. Some PMs know me for sketching in meetings — by the end of the discussion, we might walk away with rough wireframes or a flow to test whether we’re on the right track. If the problem spans multiple teams, I’ll design a workshop so we can co-create and make decisions together instead of getting stuck in silos.
3. **Align on a solution.** I usually present early solutions as storyboards or rough flows, almost like a 60-second TV commercial. It helps the group align on a clear narrative: “Here’s how we’re solving the problem, step by step.”
4. **Validate and refine.** A concept is just a hypothesis. Based on what we know, we *believe* this approach will solve the user’s problem. Next comes validation: collecting feedback from users or stakeholders to see if the idea holds up.
5. **Partner with engineering.** Once we’re confident in the direction, I collaborate with engineers on feasibility. We talk through trade-offs, constraints, and edge cases.
6. **Polish and handoff.** I refine the design artifacts so they’re build-ready — clean visuals, specs, and annotations — then hand them off to engineers.
7. **Launch and measure.** A strong launch includes go-to-market planning and analytics setup. Once the feature is live, we monitor performance, review data, and iterate. Design doesn’t stop at v1 — it’s about learning through v2, v3, and beyond.

**Concrete example:** On one project, a PM, an engineer, and I were discussing a setup flow for a complex business tracking feature. Instead of staying abstract, I grabbed some blank paper and sketched ideas right in the meeting. Within an hour, we had a rough storyboard of the user flow. That flow became the anchor for a cross-functional alignment, where we stress-tested each other’s scenarios, aligned on scope, and walked out with something we could validate and de-risk. Everyone appreciated the clarity of having a visual artifact, and we avoided weeks of circular debate.

## Lesson:

A clear, repeatable process builds trust with partners and gives your team confidence that design isn’t a black box. When you can explain how you work in plain language, people stop guessing and start collaborating.

Most aren’t afraid of the design work — they’re afraid of the *business* side that no one teaches in design school.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “What should I look out for?” It’s “How do I run freelancing like a real business so I don’t get burned?”

## Lesson hook:

As a freelancer, you’re not just a designer — you’re a business owner. Your design skills get you hired. Your business skills keep you paid, protected, and sustainable.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Congratulations on starting your freelancing journey — it *is* liberating. But that freedom comes with responsibility. The moment you take money in exchange for design work, you are running a business, whether you’re designing a menu for a local cafe or building a full website for a startup.

Start with the operational basics. Learn your local laws around business setup — taxes, expenses, invoicing, what counts as a write-off, and whether you need a sole proprietorship or LLC. It sounds intimidating, but the sooner you understand the fundamentals, the fewer headaches you’ll have later. The good news is that freelancing is a mature discipline with great resources. [AIGA](https://www.aiga.org/) offers excellent guides and contract templates. [Freelancers Union](https://www.freelancersunion.org/) is another nonprofit with support for independent workers.

2. **Terms and agreements.** You’re a service provider. You need clarity on: project scope and what’s *not* included, revision limits and change-request policies, deadlines and communication expectations, digital asset ownership and NDA/portfolio rights, how you handle late payments, and your payment structure (invoice, deposit, milestones). Never start work without a clear agreement. Ever.
3. **Legal and financial obligations.** This includes taxes, record-keeping, business expenses, and insurance. Boring, yes — but it protects you. A late invoice is annoying; a tax penalty is devastating.
4. **Common mistakes to avoid.** These are the traps I’ve seen (or lived through myself): saying yes to “quick favors” that quietly expand scope, endless revision loops because expectations weren’t set, accepting any client because you need the money (bad-fit clients cost more in stress and time), and late or unpaid invoices because payment terms weren’t clear.

You’ll get better as you take on more projects, but protecting yourself upfront saves enormous stress later.

A personal note: I prefer working for a company rather than freelancing. I don’t enjoy managing the operational side or having an unpredictable schedule. Understanding your own lifestyle preferences is part of choosing the right path.

## Lesson:

Treat freelancing like a business, not a side project. Start with fundamentals — local laws, taxes, invoicing, and business setup. Never work without a contract or clear terms. Price for sustainability, not survival. And use established resources like AIGA and Freelancers Union to build your foundation.

# What do I do if I’m the only designer and feeling lost?

Entry ID: 95
Theme: Career Navigation, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Solo designers ask this when they’re drowning without peer support. There’s no one to review their work, no one who understands their challenges, and no clear path for growth. It feels isolating and overwhelming.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Is being solo bad?” It’s “Why am I feeling lost, and is this role set up for me to succeed?”

## Lesson hook:

Being a solo designer isn’t inherently wrong — but it requires the right level of experience and the right company conditions to work.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

There’s nothing wrong with being a solo designer at an early-stage startup. But “feeling lost” is a signal that something’s misaligned — either the company isn’t ready for design, or you’re not ready to be solo.

Before you decide what to do, diagnose *why* you’re feeling lost:

1. **Is it a maturity gap?** The team doesn’t know how to work with design. They treat you like a pixel pusher, skip you in planning conversations, or constantly override your decisions with personal preferences. They don’t understand what design brings beyond making things “look nice.”
2. **Is it a growth gap?** You want to improve as a designer, but there’s no one at the company who can give you constructive feedback. You’re designing in a vacuum with no way to know if you’re getting better or building bad habits.
3. **Is it a stakeholder management gap?** The company is chaotic (common at early-stage startups), and you’re spending more time managing conflicting opinions and shifting priorities than actually designing. You haven’t built the skills yet to navigate ambiguity and influence without authority.

Each of these has a different solution:

**If it’s a maturity gap** — you need to educate the team on how to work with design. Run a “How to give design feedback” session. Involve yourself earlier in planning. Frame your work in terms they care about: business outcomes, not aesthetics. This takes energy, but it’s doable if the company is willing to learn.

**If it’s a growth gap** — seek mentorship outside of work. Join a local design community, find a mentor through ADPList or similar platforms, or join design Slack/Discord groups where you can get feedback. If your area doesn’t have a design community, this is a bigger problem — you’re not just solo at work, you’re solo in your career development.

**If it’s a stakeholder management gap** — this is a skill issue, not a company issue. Solo designers need strong communication, prioritization, and influence skills. If you’re junior or mid-level and haven’t built these muscles yet, this role will feel brutal. You might need a more structured environment with a manager who can coach you through these situations.

**When being solo works:** If you’re senior enough that design craft and stakeholder management are already solid, being a solo designer is a great opportunity. You own more scope, shape the product directly, and build influence fast. You’re not waiting for approval — you *are* the design team.

**When being solo doesn’t work:** If you’re junior or mid-level, you’re a fish out of water. You need feedback, mentorship, and a team to learn from. If your company can’t provide that *and* you can’t find it externally, you’re not growing — you’re surviving. In that case, look for a job with a design team where you can develop properly. There’s no shame in recognizing when a role isn’t set up for your success.

## Lesson:

Diagnose why you’re feeling lost: maturity gap (educate the team), growth gap (find external mentorship), or stakeholder management gap (build the skill or find a structured role). Solo designer works when you’re senior and the company is coachable. If you’re junior or mid-level without external support, find a team.

# What does good design leadership succession planning look like?

Entry ID: 91
Theme: Org Design & Ops

## Context:

People ask this when their leader is leaving or they’re worried about instability. They want to know if the transition will be chaotic or if the new leader will blow up everything they’ve built.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “What will the new leader do?” It’s “What should I expect from a leader who knows how to transition well — and what are the red flags?”

## Lesson hook:

Good succession isn’t about continuity for its own sake — it’s about honoring what works while bringing fresh direction.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

A good leadership transition should feel like something has changed, but not like a tsunami that wipes out your workflow. You want a leader who brings their own vision without bulldozing the team in the process.

Here’s what good succession looks like:

1. **They come in listening, not announcing.** A strong new leader doesn’t show up on Day 1 with a 90-day plan. They do listening tours: 1:1s with everyone on the team, sitting in on existing rituals (all-hands, design reviews, team retros), and asking “What’s working? What’s broken? What would you change if you could?” They’re gathering context before making decisions.
2. **They honor what’s working before changing what’s not.** Good leaders acknowledge the systems and relationships that already exist. They don’t immediately restructure teams or kill long-standing meetings just to leave their mark. They say things like: “I see why you do X this way — let’s keep that for now and revisit in a few months once I understand the trade-offs.”
3. **They’re transparent about their vision without forcing it immediately.** Within the first month or two, they should share their high-level priorities and working style. Not a detailed roadmap, but something like: “I care about craft quality and collaboration with product. I value async communication and clear documentation. Here’s how I run 1:1s.” You shouldn’t be guessing what they care about.
4. **They create space for questions and concerns.** Expect transition Q&As, office hours, or AMAs where people can ask: “Are you changing our org structure? Will you keep our design system team? How do you handle conflict with PMs?” A leader who avoids these conversations is usually hiding something.

**Red flags to watch for:**

- Announcing big changes in the first 30 days (restructures, new processes, team moves) without input
- Canceling long-standing rituals without understanding why they exist
- Making decisions in isolation without talking to the people affected
- Avoiding direct questions about their plans or priorities

If you’re experiencing succession, give the new leader 60–90 days before judging too harshly. But if you’re seeing red flags early and consistently, trust your gut — that’s usually how they’ll operate long-term.

## Lesson:

Good succession includes listening tours, honoring existing systems, transparent communication about vision, and space for Q&A. Red flags: big changes in the first 30 days without input, canceling rituals, avoiding tough questions.

# What is a career ladder? What does it mean for designers?

Entry ID: 71
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers hear about “levels,” “career ladders,” and “IC progression,” but few truly understand what they mean or how to use them. This creates anxiety — designers worry they’re “behind,” or they treat the ladder as a mysterious ranking instead of a tool that can actually support their growth and clarity.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “What is a career ladder?” It’s “How do I use my company’s career ladder to understand expectations and grow as a designer?”

## Lesson hook:

A career ladder is not a checklist — it’s a shared language for expectations, impact, and growth. It helps you and your leadership align on what “good” looks like at each stage of your career.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

When you work in an established tech company, you’ll almost always encounter job levels or a career ladder. A career ladder is simply the system a company uses to define expectations, pay bands, and scope of responsibility at each level. It gives employees clarity on where they stand and what growth looks like.

Each level comes with a pay range. Pay bands usually overlap — a high-level 3 might earn the same as a low-level 4 — but expectations and scope increase as you move up. That’s the key difference.

For designers, career ladders generally look something like this (titles can vary by company):

1. **Level 1 — Early in Career / Intern.** Assigned a very small feature. Works with heavy guidance. Focus is on learning fundamentals.
2. **Level 2 — Junior Designer.** Owns a small feature end-to-end with some guidance. Starts learning team processes and tools.
3. **Level 3 — Mid-Level Designer.** Owns a set of features. Expected to work with minimal guidance. Demonstrates strong craft, consistency, and reliability.
4. **Level 4 — Senior Designer.** Owns a broad, complex problem space that may span multiple product areas. Expected to operate autonomously, collaborate deeply across teams, and begin shaping design strategy with light guidance. Craft should be expert-level.
5. **Level 5 — Staff / Principal Designer (Senior+).** Owns strategy across entire product surfaces or domains. Sets vision, drives cross-functional alignment, and mentors others. Expert in craft *and* influence.

Every company structures levels slightly differently — some have Staff and Principal as separate levels, some have fewer or more — but the progression pattern is the same: **as you level up, your scope, autonomy, strategic altitude, and influence widen.**

Companies call it a “ladder” because they want employees to grow within it. A good ladder provides clarity on expectations, fairness in compensation, alignment between you and your manager, a roadmap for developing skills needed at the next stage, and a way to understand the difference between producing work vs. leading work.

If you’re curious about how different companies structure their ladders, check out [levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) for cross-company level comparisons and [staff.design](https://staff.design/) for interviews and paths of senior+ designers.

## Lesson:

A career ladder gives you clarity — it tells you what “good” looks like at each level. Use it as a tool to align with your manager on expectations and growth. As levels increase, so do autonomy, scope, and influence — not just task complexity. Your level reflects how you operate and drive outcomes, not just the artifacts you produce.

# What should I do when my design gets compromised in implementation?

Entry ID: 100
Theme: Collaboration & Influence, Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers ask this when they open the staging build and think “Wait, this isn’t what I designed.” The colors are off, the spacing is wrong, or entire interactions are missing. It’s frustrating and demoralizing.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “How do I fix what’s already broken?” It’s “Why did this happen, and how do I prevent it from happening again?”

## Lesson hook:

When your design gets compromised, it’s not about who’s wrong — it’s about where the process broke down.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

When you discover your design was implemented differently than you expected, resist the urge to blame engineering. Instead, treat it as a signal that somewhere in the process, the team lost alignment.

Here’s how to handle it:

**1. Diagnose what happened (before you react):**

Ask these questions:

- **Did requirements change mid-sprint?** Sometimes business priorities shift and the PM pivoted without telling you. Check if the scope changed after handoff.
- **Was there a feasibility issue?** Engineers might have discovered the design wasn’t technically possible (performance, platform constraints, API limits) and had to adapt on the fly.
- **Did communication break down?** Maybe they didn’t understand the design intent, misread the specs, or made assumptions when something was ambiguous.

Talk to your engineering partner directly. Say: “I noticed X got built differently than the design — can you walk me through what happened?” Most of the time, there’s a reasonable explanation.

**2. Address the immediate issue:**

Decide if it’s fixable now or later:

- **Critical quality issue** (broken user flow, accessibility fail, brand violation) — push to fix before launch. Escalate if needed.
- **Polish/refinement** (spacing slightly off, animation missing) — log it as tech debt and prioritize for the next sprint. Don’t block the launch.
- **Fundamental mismatch** (they built something completely different) — stop and regroup. This means the team wasn’t aligned on what you were building.

If it’s urgent, work with engineering to scope a quick fix. If it’s not blocking, move it to the backlog and keep momentum.

**3. Prevent it from happening again:**

Run a lightweight retrospective with the team. Ask:

- **Could we have caught this earlier?** Did we do a design walkthrough? Did engineering see the designs before they started coding? Could we have built a quick prototype to validate feasibility?
- **Were the specs clear enough?** Did the handoff doc cover edge cases, interactions, and responsive behavior? Did we annotate enough, or were they guessing?
- **Is this a recurring pattern?** If designs consistently get compromised, the root cause is systemic: unclear requirements, tight timelines, lack of design review during implementation, or poor collaboration rhythm.

Then adjust your process: schedule a mid-sprint check-in to catch issues early, do a design walkthrough before engineering starts (not just after handoff), and build stronger relationships with your engineering partners so they feel comfortable flagging concerns.

## Lesson:

When your design gets compromised, diagnose why it happened (requirements changed, feasibility issue, communication breakdown). Fix critical issues now, backlog polish for later. Run a retro to prevent it from recurring — this is a process problem, not a people problem.

# What’s different between working in agency vs. product company?

Entry ID: 35
Theme: Career Navigation, Industry & Trends

## Context:

Designers often ask whether they should start in an agency or join a product company. Both paths give you valuable experience, but the nature of the work and lifestyle can be very different.

## Reframe:

The better question isn’t “Which one is better?” but “Which environment matches my career stage, learning goals, and lifestyle preferences?”

## Lesson hook:

Agency = variety and speed. Product company = depth and ownership.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

**Agency work:**

1. **Project-based.** Engagements are fixed-time (often 3-4 months) and client-driven.
2. **Breadth of exposure.** You get to work on many industries, products, and concepts — a great way to learn quickly if you’re still exploring.
3. **Creative focus.** Agencies are often brought in for concepting, branding, or high-visibility design.
4. **Tradeoffs.** You rarely see the end-to-end product journey, and the hours can be intense to meet client deadlines. Work-life balance may be a challenge.

**Product company work:**

1. **One brand / set of products.** You go deep instead of wide, building expertise over time.
2. **Ownership.** You shape and sustain the full experience of a product, not just a concept phase.
3. **Stability.** Roles tend to be steadier since they’re not tied to short-term contracts.
4. **Tradeoffs.** Less variety compared to agency life; you’ll often be refining within one domain for longer stretches.

## Lesson:

If you thrive on variety, speed, and exploration, agency life can be exciting early in your career. If you prefer depth, ownership, and long-term impact, product companies may be a better fit. Many designers benefit from trying both at different points in their career.

# What’s normal career progression for a designer?

Entry ID: 96
Theme: Career Navigation

## Context:

Designers ask this when they’re trying to benchmark themselves against some imagined timeline — worried they’re behind, or unsure what they should be working toward next.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Am I on track?” It’s “What does progression actually look like, and which path makes sense for me?”

## Lesson hook:

Career progression in design isn’t a single ladder — it’s a ladder that forks, and both paths lead somewhere legitimate.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Design careers generally follow four levels: junior, mid, senior, and senior+. The first two are mostly about climbing craft and strategic thinking — learning how to execute well, then learning how to think beyond the brief.

Senior is where it gets interesting, because most companies will present you with a fork: stay as an individual contributor and go deeper, or move into people management.

**The IC track** — Senior+ titles like Staff, Principal, or Lead mean you’re no longer just owning a product area. You’re looking across a product family or an entire ecosystem. Your scope expands, but your currency is still craft and strategic influence, not headcount.

**The management track** — This is a completely different career, not a promotion. The skills required — coaching, developing people, navigating org dynamics — are fundamentally different from design craft. It’s the right path for people who are genuinely energized by growing others, not for people who think it’s the next logical step up.

If you’re considering the fork, ask yourself honestly: Do I want to be great at design, or do I want to help others become great designers? Both are valid. But conflating them leads to unhappy managers and underserved teams.

On pace: most designers spend about 5–8 years as ICs before the fork becomes real. But that’s a range, not a rule. Progression also depends on the company — startups tend to move faster, larger enterprises slower. And at some point, you have to factor in what matters to you personally. Someone prioritizing family, stability, or outside interests might move more slowly, and that’s a choice, not a failure.

## Lesson:

Don’t measure yourself against someone else’s timeline. Understand the fork, know which path fits your values, and progress at a pace that’s sustainable for your whole life — not just your career.

# What’s one underrated skill that makes a big difference in design careers?

Entry ID: 59
Theme: Mindset & Growth

## Context:

Designers often obsess over tools, frameworks, or craft techniques — but the differentiator between *good* and *great* careers usually isn’t technical. It’s how well you communicate your thinking. Mentees ask this when they feel stuck — they’re delivering good work, but struggling to make it *land*.

## Reframe:

The question isn’t “What new skill should I learn?” — it’s “Which skill amplifies everything I already know?”

## Lesson hook:

That skill is **storytelling.**

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Design is, at its core, communication. We take complexity, organize it, and express it clearly — through flows, systems, and visuals. Storytelling is how that clarity travels through the organization.

When I look at career trajectories, I see a clear pattern:

- Designers who *don’t* tell stories well often become feature factories — they wait for PMs to define requirements and then deliver specs.
- Designers who *do* tell stories well become partners — they guide teams toward better decisions, align stakeholders, and earn trust.

Storytelling shows up everywhere in design:

- In critique, when you help others understand *why* a decision matters.
- In design reviews, when you frame trade-offs and invite collaboration.

## Lesson:

Storytelling turns design from output into influence. It’s the difference between being the person who takes orders and the one who shapes direction. Practice it until it feels natural — because great designers design experiences, but great design leaders design stories people believe in.

# What’s something you think most portfolios get wrong?

Entry ID: 17
Theme: Portfolio & Interviewing

## Context:

Most early-career designers stress over “filling” a portfolio with the right ingredients: personas, flows, wireframes, prototypes. They assume if they check every box, they’ll look hireable.

## Reframe:

Hiring managers don’t hire artifacts — they hire problem-solvers. A portfolio is only effective if it shows how you think, how you make decisions, and how your work creates impact.

## Lesson hook:

Portfolios don’t need to look full — they need to look sharp.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

The most common mistake I see is what I call “UX theatre”: personas for persona’s sake, flowcharts for flowchart’s sake. These artifacts show you can follow the playbook, but they don’t prove you can solve problems. What stands out is when your work connects the dots clearly: *“Target users behave this way, therefore I designed with this consideration in mind.”*

Another mistake is overloading with quantity. Reviewers skim portfolios in ~3 minutes. Six projects where four are weak dilute the strong ones and signal poor judgment. As a hiring manager, I assume the lowest-quality project you include is your quality bar.

A stronger approach:

1. **Lead with 2 polished case studies** that clearly show problem → process → impact.
2. **Add 1–2 lighter explorations** if they show range, but frame them as supporting work.
3. **Cut anything** that doesn’t reflect the designer you want to be hired as.

## Lesson:

Portfolios don’t need to prove you can make deliverables. They need to prove you can think critically, make tradeoffs, and deliver impact.

# What’s the difference between design maturity in companies

Entry ID: 27
Theme: Career Navigation, Industry & Trends

## Context:

Designers often feel frustrated by how differently companies treat design. That’s usually a reflection of the company’s *design maturity* — how well-equipped they are to deliver user-centered design.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “What’s the maturity model?” but “How will maturity shape my role as a designer here?”

## Lesson hook:

Maturity changes whether you’re fighting for design to exist, or building on design as the norm.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

This is a very important decision to make when you’re choosing a new job or even switching teams within a company. Each organization operates at a different maturity level — and the key is picking one that matches your current career stage and focus.

Matching the maturity level to *your* career stage makes all the difference — sometimes you need the hustle of a low-maturity org, sometimes you need the platform of a high-maturity one. # What’s the right balance of visuals vs. words in case studies?

Entry ID: 38
Theme: Portfolio & Interviewing

## Context:

Designers often struggle with how much to *write* in their case studies. Too much text looks overwhelming, but too few words leave reviewers guessing what happened.

## Reframe:

The real question isn’t “Should I add more visuals or more words?” but “How can I communicate my process so it’s scannable, clear, and memorable?”

## Lesson hook:

Words explain *why*, visuals show *how*. You need both — but in the right proportions.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

When I review portfolios, here’s what works:

1. **Visuals anchor the story.** Show flows, wireframes, prototypes, screenshots. These give reviewers quick evidence of your craft.
2. **Words create clarity.** A few sentences per section: the problem, your role, key decisions, and outcomes. Think captions, not essays.
3. **Use hierarchy.** Headlines for “Problem → Process → Outcome.” Short bullet points underneath. This makes your case study scannable in under 3 minutes.
4. **Make your role explicit.** A short sentence like *“My role: led research, co-designed flows, collaborated with 2 engineers.”* Without this, it’s impossible to assess contribution.
5. **Outcomes matter most.** Even if a project didn’t launch, call out what you learned or what impact it had internally.

What not to do:

- Walls of text — reviewers won’t read them. I certainly don’t read word for word.
- Pixel dumps — images without context feel like decoration, not design.
- Generic team statements — “we did X” hides your role. What did *you* do?

For more information, check out my Medium article: [My Portfolio Evaluation Process](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/my-portfolio-evaluation-process-342005398262)

## Lesson:

A strong case study feels like a storyboard: visuals draw people in, words make the meaning clear. If you can tell the full story in 3–5 minutes of skimming, you’ve hit the right balance.

## Reframe:

The better question isn’t *“Should I speak up or listen?”* It’s *“How do I engage in a way that adds value to the conversation?”*

## Lesson hook:

Silence isn’t neutrality — it’s absence.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

My advice: don’t sit quietly until the meeting ends, especially if you have questions. Engage in the discussion as it happens.

- **Frame the team as one team.** You’re not in a waterfall where requirements are handed down. You’re a partner shaping the solution together.

Do you have to speak up every time? No. There are meetings where the goal is to absorb context — like a back-end engineering review or a business update. But in most cross-functional discussions, I aim to speak up about 90% of the time. That way, I’m actively participating, clarifying alignment, and helping shape project direction.

## Lesson:

As a designer, your role in meetings isn’t to quietly take notes — it’s to actively shape the conversation. Ask, clarify, contribute. Your presence in the room isn’t just to observe — it’s to shape.

# When did you last disagree with PM/engineering? How did you handle it?

Entry ID: 9
Theme: Collaboration & Influence

## Context:

Disagreements in cross-functional work are normal — and healthy. They’re not about “winning” but about sharpening ideas and aligning on the best path forward.

## Reframe:

The better question isn’t *“How do I avoid conflict?”* but *“How do I use disagreements to improve the product and the team’s alignment?”*

## Lesson hook:

Good disagreements sharpen the work, not the relationship.

## Answer/Joy’s personal perspective:

Disagreements happen fairly often, and my next step is always to talk them through and find common ground. I expect PMs and engineers to explain their reasoning, and I share mine. Then we come back to: *What are we trying to accomplish together?*

**Example — UI trade-off:**

As a team of Eng/PM/Design, we debated how to display data in a line graph. The real question was: what story are we telling?

- **Option 1:** Daily counts show activity → “this technology is working.”
- **Option 2:** Accumulative trend shows impact → “this is the long-term value.”

I leaned toward impact, since activity alone can feel superficial. The PM brought in customer insights suggesting activity was more important for this context. In the end, we went with the PM’s direction — but also agreed to surface the impact view elsewhere.

**Example — pushback without rationale:**

Sometimes pushback comes as a “hunch” without evidence. In those cases, I hold my ground more firmly, using design-system reasoning, user needs, and best practices to explain why the design holds.

## Lesson:

The key is balance: flex when others bring strong evidence, stand firm when the pushback is ungrounded. Either way, treat disagreements as a way to refine the product, not as battles to win.
